Carnegie Kitchen App

CMU Fall 2019
Duration: 8 Weeks
Skills: UI/UX, Wire-framing, User Research, Visual Branding

Collaborators: Angela Lee, Gretchen Kupferschmid
Role: Conducted surveys, peer/staff interviews, and behavior tracking studies. Designed, wireframed, and prototyped the Carnegie Kitchen app interface. My collaborators designed all the illustrative assets .

Overview

“How might we improve nutritional literacy among Carnegie Mellon students in regards to both on-campus and off-campus dining?”

Design Process

Throughout these 8 weeks, my team and I divided our research and design process into three primary categories:

01 / Problem Space

As second year CMU students who love food and priority healthy eating, we wanted to understand students and their relationship with nutrition. Because of that, we asked the question,



“How might we improve nutritional literacy among Carnegie Mellon students in regards to both on-campus and off-campus dining ?”

Problem

As students passionate about healthy living, we noticed the lack of nutritional information surrounding CMU’s dining options. There are a few CMU digital and physical resources dedicated to nutritional convenience / wellbeing (ex: dining calendars, nutritional calculator, health services). While these resources exist, these resources are scattered across multiple platforms making it inconvenient to access them on a regular basis.

As a result, students don’t take advantage of what CMU provides, resulting in an inability to achieve a good balance between health and academics at CMU. Many are constantly stressed, unhappy, and unaware of how they can use food to improve their wellbeing.



Solution

Carnegie Kitchen, a nutrition app that subtly encourages behavior changes by integrating habit-building nudges with practical dining functions.

02 / Stakeholder Mapping

Though we had begun to list out the main stakeholders involved in our problem space, we fleshed out the relationships and hierarchy within possible other stakeholders through information mapping. After continuing to unpack stakeholders (like students), we started to categorize them based on whether they were internal or external in regards to CMU. From doing so, we realized that some stakeholders don’t fit completely in one category and should be treated more flexibly. Flushing out this stakeholder map revealed to us the wide scope of different participant in the CMU food ecosystem.

Visualizing the numerous types of CMU eaters

03 / User Research

Overview

Over the course of two weeks, we gather a total of over +600 responses from over +150 unique profiles. Forms of research we conducted include:


01 / Nutritional Literacy Survey

02 / Behavior Tracking

03 / Top Dining Location Survey

04 / CMU Dining Painpoint Survey

05 / Staff Interviews

06 / Eating Stress Factors Survey


Student's Nutritional Literacy [Survey]

Overall, the majority had some sort of misconception about what it means to have a positive relationship with food. Furthermore, many people didn’t care about their nutrition and for those who did care, they felt a lot of negativity/guilt when it came to being “healthy”. Many also reported that they felt hyper focused on certain measures of nutrition rather than looking at nutrition from a holistic perspective.


User data organized by school year & student's self-proclaimed health prioritization

Five-day Student Food Log [Behavior Tracking]

Over the course of seven days, my collaborators and I tracked our eight student's diets plus ourselves. We documented details such as, type of food, reason of choice, and satisfaction. We found that convenience and preference played the biggest role in dining decisions across almost every meal. It didn't matter how much a dish was desired, if it was cold outside and far to walk to, individuals didn't go.

We intentionally tracked students of varying degrees of nutritional habits and outlooks on health

Top Dining Locations [Survey] — 51 Individuals

We found that the top five dining options on campus were in the University Center (ABP + Entropy), Tepper Quad (Taza de Oro), Resnik (Taste of India + Innovation Kitchen + Melt Lab), and Posner Hall (The Exchange). After mapping the survey points visually, we realized that the biggest factors, again were location, convenience, and preference of taste.

Results showed that the University Center was the most popular

CMU Dining Painpoints [Survey]

From this survey, we found that people tend to be frustrated with the convenience of getting their food as much of the lack of nutritional literacy. Many also feel like there aren't enough cultural foods that are familiar to how they eat back at home. There was an overall lack of trust in CMU Dining's ingredients and cooking practices.

Data categorized by the type of pain point

Final Designs

Overview

In our final app intervention, we made sure to cater towards the wide spectrum of CMU students and their dietary needs. The primary features we decided to design for include:


01 / Living Dining Calendar

02 / Pre-order System

03 / Recipe Exploring + Sharing

04 / User Profile Hub

05 / CMU Health Services

06 / Emotional Check-ins

Live Dining — Calendar

The primary function for this app is the live dining calendar for on-campus restaurants. This already exists on CMU’s website as well as on a student-built site called CMU Eats, but the issue right now is a lot of valuable information on CMU dining is scattered across the web and across various digital services.

This feature is important to the app because it’s the practical feature that will keep students consistently using the app, and ultimately lead them to our more important features, which will be discussed later. From this page, users are able to explore individual dining locations and view individual restaurant items.

Live Dining — Nutrition Facts

Viewing nutrition facts can contribute to obsessive nutrient tracking behaviors, so we designed the app to utilize a precautionary educational barrier that asks you to watch a awareness video before accessing macro tables.

Living Dining - Preorder System

One of the biggest priorities we found among students was convenience and time. Introducing a pre-order system addresses this concern, ultimately helping students have a more stress-free and efficient day.

Recipe Page

For people who sometimes or fully eat off campus, we added a recipe viewing and sharing aspect. We didn’t, however want to create a social media platform, so you can simply save recipes, but not see the quantity of saves- didn’t want to contribute to the negative aspects of social media and nutrition, all about maintaining privacy, but creating a community at the same time. On each individual recipe page, users are able to view crucial information relevant to the recipe.

User Profile Page

Obviously, nutrition is highly dependent on the individual and their eating tendencies. Because of that, Carnegie Kitchen allows users to save and share recipes, and access saved videos from the Tips n' Tricks page.

Tips N' Tricks

Because our research revealed people don't have a healthy outlook on what properly nourishing oneself means, we designed Carnegie Kitchen to have a video resources page. This page provides practical videos that include emotion-based videos to establish the role nutrition and foods play in shaping one's emotional health. This feature is also meant to help people to move from thinking of food in terms of appearance to nutrition as a way of improving mental wellbeing.

Emotional Check-ins

Food has an emotional effect on all of us, especially for vulnerable eaters. Because this aspect of eating is often overlooked, we integrated occasional emotional banner check-ins. If the app notices that a user has been feeling down lately, we help them process and respond to their emotions in a non-destructive ways, such as talking to CMU services, watching emotional resources, and an external link to some food memes. :)

CMU Health Services

From our interviews with both students and staff, we found that there is lack of relationship between CMU services, not because CMU services aren’t good, but because students don’t know about it. The app has an entire page dedicated to CMU Health Services in hoeps of building a stronger relationship between the two parties through access and knowledge.
