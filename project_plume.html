Plume System

Over the course of 4 months, I along with several HCI undergrads/grads worked with a group of +30 Electrical Computer Engineering students on developing Plume, a five-part IoT system geared towards improving the lives of Parkinson's Disease patients.

As part of the Interactions team, I led Phase 2, the design and prototyping phase of the project. More specifically, I worked on the CUI (Alexa Skill) component of the Plume system.

Overview

Problem

On a daily basis, PD patients battle numerous motor and physical problems including hand tremor, gait, memory, lack of motivation, and emotional sadness. These struggles not only make each day more difficult, but can lead to extreme depression and extreme physical injuries.

Currently, physicians must rely on infrequent visits to develop treatment plans and assess patients’ needs. This prevents an accurate understanding of what a patient’s true experience is at home.



Solution

Plume — a IoT product that bridges the gap between patients, caregivers, and doctors through integration of VR exercise, CUI assistance, movement/gait detection, and data visualization.

*NOTE: the Plume system addresses tackles almost every aspect of a PD patient's struggles. The ideal implemenation for an actual patient would be a selected combination of the proposed solution.

Voiceflow Prototyping

Using Voiceflow, my team built and tested out each conversation flow through an actual Amazon Echo. We focused on generating plausible user responses and refining the naturalness of Alexa's returns.

Given that our CUI integrates sentiment analysis and item-detection sensors, features that aren't present in any voice prototyping tool, we decided to manually translate these to conversational flow maps for team hand off.

Medication Reminder

From our interviews with physicians and patients, we found that promptly taking medication is a pervasive issue for PD patients. Through integration with Google Calendar, the Alexa Plume Skill notifies users at designated times every day.

Emotional Check-in

Though designed for evenings, the patient has the option to enter a reflective mode with Alexa at any given time. Throughout this function, Plume not only gives the patient an opportunity to verbally share, but also analyzes the patient’s emotional state with a LSTM-based neural net. Given the patient’s consent, this information is then sent to the doctor’s dashboard for evaluation purposes.

Props to Elizabeth for leading this hefty part of the design

Post-activity Evaluations

As a research method, the Plume skill conducts occasional event debriefs in hopes of helping doctors understand what types of activities most benefit the well-being of patients. During this conversation, Plume detects the dominant emotional response through a LSTM-based neural net and reaffirms that through verbal conversation.

Originally, Alexa would send a notification through RFID tracking when a patient re-entered the house. Though emotional detection works, the sensors teams was unable to technically integrate the tracking feature into this prototype due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Tile Detection

An important component of the Plume voice assistant is its ability to integrate with Tile Bluetooth tracking. This feature helps alleviate memory difficulties that often come from Parkinson's disease and old age.
